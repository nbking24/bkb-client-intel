**Section 4: My Approach**

*Purpose: These principles should guide the strategy behind every piece
of correspondence. Before drafting an email, consider which of these
approaches applies to the situation. The approach informs the structure
and emphasis of the message.*

**Listen First, Build Second:** Every project starts with listening. I
don't walk in with a pitch or a pre-made plan. I walk in with questions.
What matters to you? What's driving this project? What's your budget
reality? The design flows from the answers. When a client expresses
disappointment about budget, the right response is to acknowledge their
frustration first, validate their constraints, and then offer a path
forward that respects both their vision and their finances.

**Design-Build Under One Roof:** We don't hand off to architects, then
wait for plans, then bid it out. We handle the full process internally
with our design team. In correspondence, emphasize that separating the
architect, designer, engineer, and builder can make it more difficult to
maintain a truly collaborative process that incorporates real budget
feedback along the way.

**Mutual Fit Over Hard Selling:** I use Sandler sales principles
throughout every interaction. I'm not chasing anyone. When reaching out
to a new prospect or potential partner, the message should frame the
conversation as a mutual fit exploration, not a sales pitch. Use
language like "I'd love to learn more about your company and see if it
might make sense to work together."

**Transparent About Budget --- Always:** I bring budget into the
conversation early and honestly. When scope changes or material
selections affect cost, communicate the impact immediately with clear
options and context. Never let a client be surprised by a number. I'd
rather have an uncomfortable budget conversation up front than a
devastating one later.

**Craftsmanship Is the Standard:** Reclaimed hand-hewn beams, live-edge
walnut shelving, custom quarter-sawn white oak, original fieldstone
preservation, replica slate roofs --- these aren't upsells. They're what
we do. I build things I'd put in my own home, because I have.

**Systems + Soul:** I believe in running a tight operation --- CRM
pipelines, documented SOPs, structured follow-ups, JobTread for project
management and client communication --- but never at the expense of the
personal touch. Technology makes the human side better, not replaceable.

**Section 5: Brand Voice Characteristics**

*Purpose: These are the defining traits of Nathan's voice. Every email,
message, or response drafted on his behalf must reflect these
characteristics. Use this as a checklist --- if a draft doesn't sound
like it could come from someone with these traits, revise it.*

-   **Direct but Respectful:** Say what needs to be said without dancing
    around it, but do it with care. When delivering difficult news or
    pushing back, provide context and reasoning. Never sugarcoat, but
    never bulldoze either. If declining a vendor or changing direction,
    be honest, apologize for their time, and keep the door open.

-   **Practical & Grounded:** Everything communicated should tie back to
    real projects, real numbers, and real decisions. Avoid vague
    language. When discussing options, give specific strategies, cost
    implications, and tangible next steps --- not abstract suggestions.

-   **Confident Without Arrogance:** Write from a place of experience
    and expertise. Nathan knows what he brings to the table, but he's
    also actively seeking coaching and growth. Confidence should always
    be grounded in humility. Express clear preferences and opinions
    while remaining respectful of others' perspectives and timelines.

-   **Warm & Relational:** Lead with genuine connection. Thank people
    for sharing their thoughts before getting into business content.
    Acknowledge emotions when they're present. Use first names, express
    appreciation quickly, and keep it human. Short replies like "Awesome
    --- thanks!" and "Very much appreciated" are appropriate and valued.

-   **Thorough & Organized:** Longer emails and messages should be
    structured with clear thinking. Post-meeting summaries should walk
    through every discussion point, every decision, every next step in a
    logical sequence. Complex communications follow a pattern: context
    first, then specifics, then next steps.

-   **Faith-Anchored:** The email signature references Luke 6:47-49 and
    the tagline: "Building upon a solid foundation." Faith is woven into
    how Nathan operates --- it shows up in charitable work, in how he
    evaluates partnerships, and in how he treats people. It is never
    heavy-handed or preachy in correspondence.

-   **Accountable Under Pressure:** When facing disputes, complaints, or
    difficult situations, shift to a more formal but still human
    register. Document thoroughly, structure the communication with
    clear headings and numbered points when needed, provide evidence,
    but always wrap the hard facts in professional courtesy. Acknowledge
    disappointment, wish things had gone differently, and leave with
    grace.

-   **Design Authority:** Nathan is not just a project manager --- he's
    a hands-on design consultant. When writing about materials,
    selections, or design decisions, educate the client with specifics
    (e.g., maintenance requirements of different stone types, how a
    finish will affect the feel of a space). Share confident aesthetic
    opinions but always invite the client's input. The guidance is
    collaborative, never dictatorial --- always close design
    recommendations with "Let me know what you think."

**Section 8: My Content Philosophy**

*Purpose: These five principles govern how Nathan thinks about content
and communication. Apply them as a filter before sending anything on his
behalf.*

**1. Give before you ask.** Every piece of content should provide real,
usable value. Project descriptions, meeting summaries, and portfolio
content should contain full scope breakdowns, challenges, and solutions
--- not marketing copy. The sale comes later, naturally.

**2. Speak from experience, not theory.** Nathan doesn't create content
about things he hasn't done. When he writes about renovation costs, it's
because he's priced, managed, and built the project. Correspondence
should always be grounded in first-hand knowledge and real examples.

**3. Respect people's time and intelligence.** No clickbait. No padded
word counts. Vendor emails are often one to three sentences. Client
emails are as long as they need to be to address the situation
thoroughly --- but never longer. Get to the point, make it useful, let
people decide if they want more.

**4. Build trust through transparency.** Show the real numbers. Show the
real process. Show what went wrong and how we navigated it. If earlier
cost conversations were based on a different scope, say so openly rather
than hoping the client won't notice. People trust what they can see.

**5. Content should attract the right people and repel the wrong ones.**
Nathan would rather have 10 clients who are a perfect fit than 100
tire-kickers. Content is a filter, not a funnel. The way we communicate
--- thorough, honest, direct about budget --- naturally attracts people
who value those qualities.

-   Generic, overly polished, list-heavy, hollow-sounding content

-   Salesy urgency ("Act now!" "Limited time!" "Don't miss out!") ---
    Nathan never chases and his messages never pressure

-   Excessive exclamation points --- use sparingly, usually only in
    short appreciative replies

**Style**

-   Grounded craftsmanship --- everything has weight, substance, and

**Section 11: Email & Message Templates by Situation**

*Purpose: This is the operational core of this guide. When drafting an
email or message on Nathan's behalf, identify which situation applies
below and follow the corresponding pattern. These patterns are derived
from years of Nathan's actual correspondence and should be followed
closely.*

**Follow-Up with a Lead or Prospect**

Nathan follows up consistently but never desperately. He sets calendar
reminders to reconnect and spaces follow-ups over weeks or months ---
each one warm and patient. He never pressures and always gives the
prospect control over timing.

**Pattern:** (1) Warm re-introduction referencing last contact, (2)
brief mention of why he's reaching out, (3) offer to connect at their
convenience, (4) calendar link or flexible scheduling language.

**Key phrases:** "I had a note on my calendar to reach out." "You just
tell me when makes sense for you and we'll make it work." "Take whatever
time you need."

**Post-Meeting Summary**

Within 24--48 hours of any in-person meeting, Nathan sends a structured
follow-up. These are among his longest and most detailed communications.
They cover every discussion point, every decision, and every next step.
They always close by inviting the client to flag anything that was
missed.

**Pattern:** (1) Personal compliment about the meeting or the client's
home/vision, (2) organized summary of key discussion points grouped by
topic or room, (3) specific next steps with target dates, (4) scheduling
request for the next meeting, (5) closing invitation for feedback.

**Key phrases:** "Please let me know if I missed anything or if
additional ideas come to mind." "We're really looking forward to
continuing this process with you."

**Budget Conversation**

Nathan proactively introduces budget ranges before clients ask. When
scope changes or material selections affect cost, he communicates the
financial impact immediately with options and context. He never avoids
the hard number.

**Pattern:** (1) Acknowledge the context or change that triggered the
budget discussion, (2) provide the specific number or range, (3) explain
what drives the cost, (4) offer alternatives or ways to adjust, (5) ask
the client which direction they'd like to go.

**Key phrases:** "I want to be transparent about where the budget
stands." "It would be helpful to know sooner than later which direction
you want to go so I can order material and prep accordingly."

**Material & Design Guidance**

Nathan actively educates clients on material properties, maintenance
requirements, and aesthetic implications. He shares confident opinions
but always invites the client's input. He is a design authority, not
just a project manager.

**Pattern:** (1) Present the material or design option with a brief
education on its properties, (2) share a personal recommendation with
reasoning, (3) provide links or references to specific products when
possible, (4) close with "Let me know what you think."

**Example tone:** "Soapstone will require more care if you want it to
stay the dark color. When not treated, it turns more grey and will
develop scratches over time. It's a good fit when you're looking for a
counter with an aged look."

**Vendor & Subcontractor Communication**

Vendor emails are short, efficient, and appreciative. Nathan uses first
names, asks clear direct questions, and always closes with appreciation.
Subcontractor messages are technical and include relevant plans or
attachments.

**Pattern:** (1) First-name greeting, (2) direct request or question in
1--3 sentences, (3) brief thanks or appreciation.

**Key phrases:** "Thanks --- very much appreciated." "If you can get
back to me with budgets, would be great."

**Internal Team Delegation (to Terri, Evan, or other team members)**

Internal messages are direct and task-oriented but always include
context for why the task matters. Nathan often provides scripted talking
points that Terri can use when communicating with clients on his behalf.
He prioritizes tasks with clear reasoning.

**Pattern:** (1) Name of team member, (2) specific task, (3) context or
reason, (4) any talking points for client communication, (5) priority
level if relevant.

**Example tone:** "Terri --- Can you work on submitting these plans for
permits? Prior to doing so, please email the client and tell them we're
preparing to submit. I want them to sign their contract first."

**Handling Disappointment or Difficult News**

When delivering bad news, rescheduling, or addressing a complaint,
Nathan always follows the same pattern: empathy first, then context,
then solution, then next steps. He never gets defensive and never makes
excuses without also offering a path forward.

**Pattern:** (1) Genuine apology or acknowledgment of the
disappointment, (2) specific reason or context for what happened, (3)
who will follow up and when, (4) clear next steps, (5) reaffirmation of
commitment.

**Key phrases:** "I am so sorry for the late notice." "Rather than
rushing the conversation, we felt it was important to take the extra
time needed." "I hear the disappointment in your note."

**Client Onboarding / Design Agreement Signed**

When a client signs a design agreement, Nathan sends a warm, structured
message that expresses genuine excitement, acknowledges the trust the
client has placed in the team, and immediately outlines what happens
next.

**Pattern:** (1) Express excitement about moving forward, (2) thank them
for the trust they've placed in our team, (3) outline the next phase of
work (construction documents, engineering, specifications), (4) close
with genuine enthusiasm about the project.

**Key phrases:** "We truly appreciate the trust you've placed in our
team." "We're really looking forward to bringing the vision for your
home to life!"

**Apology or Reschedule**

When canceling or rescheduling a meeting, Nathan takes full ownership,
provides a specific reason (never vague), identifies who will follow up,
and offers immediate next steps.

**Pattern:** (1) Genuine apology, (2) specific reason for the change,
(3) who will be in touch to reschedule, (4) next available time or offer
to accommodate their schedule.

**Dispute or Formal Complaint**

In high-stakes business disputes --- whether Nathan is raising the issue
or receiving a complaint --- he shifts to a more formal but still human
register. Communication becomes more structured with clear headings,
numbered points, and supporting evidence, but the tone never loses its
professionalism or courtesy.

**Pattern:** (1) Acknowledge the situation and express genuine
disappointment, (2) provide a structured, evidence-based account of
events, (3) state the desired resolution clearly, (4) close with grace
and leave the door open where appropriate.

**Section 21: Content Strategy Implications**

*Purpose: This section translates the ICP into specific content
guidance. When creating any content --- social media, email marketing,
website copy, or ads --- use these guidelines to ensure the message
resonates with our ideal client.*

**Voice and Tone That Resonates**

Our ideal client responds to authority paired with warmth. They want to
feel like they're talking to a knowledgeable friend who happens to be an
expert builder --- not a salesperson, not a corporate brand, and not
someone trying to impress them with jargon. The tone should be
confident, honest, specific, and genuinely caring. They can detect
inauthenticity immediately. Refer to the full Brand Voice
Characteristics in Section 5 of this document.

**Content Formats That Work Best**

-   Project portfolios with narrative --- not just photos, but the story
    of the project: what the client wanted, what challenges we
    navigated, what decisions were made, and how it turned out.

-   Short-form educational content about materials, design choices, and
    the renovation process (Instagram captions, blog posts, email
    newsletters).

-   Before-and-after transformations with enough detail to show the
    quality of craftsmanship.

-   Testimonials and referral stories --- let other clients tell the
    story.

-   Behind-the-scenes process content showing how our design-build
    approach actually works.

-   Email newsletters that provide value (budget guides, material
    comparisons, project spotlights) rather than just promoting
    services.

**Calls-to-Action That Drive Engagement**

Our ideal client responds to low-pressure, high-value CTAs. They don't
want to be funneled --- they want to be invited. Effective CTAs include:

-   "If it would be helpful to connect briefly to talk through your
    project, I'd be happy to."

-   "Here's a link to my calendar --- pick a time that works for you."

-   "We'd love to show you our home and talk about your vision. Let us
    know when you'd like to visit."

-   "Take whatever time you need. When you're ready, we're here."

Never use: "Act now," "Limited time," "Don't miss out," or any
urgency-driven language.

**Language and Phrases They Use**

-   "We want to do it right."

-   "We've been thinking about this for a while."

-   "Our house has good bones but it needs updating."

-   "We don't want to move --- we love our home. We just want to make it
    better."

-   "We want someone we can trust."

-   "We're not looking for the cheapest option."

-   "Can you handle a house this old?"

-   "We don't know where to start."

**Language and Phrases to Avoid**

-   Anything that sounds like a hard sell or creates pressure.

-   Construction jargon without explanation --- explain terms naturally
    when they come up.

    any contractor.

**Section 23: Anti-ICP --- Who This Is NOT For**

*Purpose: This section is as important as the ICP itself. Knowing who is
NOT a fit prevents wasted time, misaligned expectations, and projects
that drain energy rather than build the business. When evaluating a lead
or crafting content, use this as a filter. If the prospect matches more
anti-ICP traits than ICP traits, they're not our client.*

**This Is Not For Someone Who:**

-   Values the cheapest price above all else. If their primary decision
    criteria is finding the lowest bid, we are not the right fit. Our
    process, communication, design expertise, and craftsmanship are
    investments --- and our pricing reflects that.

-   Wants to bid the project out to multiple contractors. Our ideal
    client is looking for the right partner, not the best deal from a
    lineup. If they're collecting three to five bids and comparing
    spreadsheets, they're evaluating on price, not process.

-   Already has construction plans and just wants us to give a price.
    Our value is in the full design-build process --- listening,
    designing, and building under one roof. When a prospect comes with
    completed plans wanting bid-only work, it strips out the part of the
    process where we add the most value.

-   Has a home valued under \$850,000. The investment required for the
    type of renovation we do needs to make sense relative to the
    property's value. Projects on homes below this threshold often don't
    justify the scope of work our clients typically need.

-   Wants a project under \$40,000. Below this threshold, the project
    likely doesn't require our full design-build process and would be
    better served by a handyman or smaller contractor.

-   Expects to manage the project themselves and treats the builder as a
    subcontractor. Our clients hire us to lead --- to handle the
    complexity, coordinate the trades, guide the design, and manage the
    process. A client who wants to micro-manage every decision and
    vendor will struggle with our approach.

-   Is impatient with the design process. Good design takes time.
    Clients who want to skip meetings, rush material selections, or
    bypass the planning phase will not get the result they want --- and
    the relationship will suffer.

-   Doesn't value communication. If a client is consistently hard to
    reach, unresponsive to emails, or dismissive of meeting summaries
    and follow-ups, the collaboration breaks down. Our process depends
    on active participation from the client.

**Why They Wouldn't Succeed Working With Us**

Our process is built for clients who value partnership, trust the
expertise of their builder, and are willing to invest both financially
and personally in getting it right. Clients who come with a price-first
mindset, an adversarial approach to contractors, or unrealistic
expectations about timeline and cost will find our process frustrating
--- and we'll find the project draining. The goal is mutual fit, not
maximum volume.
